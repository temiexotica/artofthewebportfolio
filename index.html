Week 2

Ulises Carrion's Selection from Second Thought emphasized the need to challenge traditional structures of language, texts, and meaning. What grasped my attention the most was the modifications he proposed for books, especially because those books already existed. This further proved his point that the meaning of text is not fixed and can always be re-contextualized. I was confused with the discourse about novels being boring yet poetry isn't?
Week 3

I really enjoyed Laurel Schwulst’s piece. With the freedoms that online technological advances provides, also comes the constrains of users feeling the need to rely on big corporations such as Instagram, Twitter, Google etc. From her piece, I took away that true website liberation is through creating “individual nodes breathing life into a vast network”. With more and more users having anxieties about not keeping up with the digital world, the internet loses its different multi-faceted spheres. When she mentioned that personal websites give room for clarity and intent, I was able to form a connection to Edouard U’s essay. Rejecting constraints both in the material and digital world is what gives things meaning.
Week 4

Because of our advancement into the post-digital world, publishing has had to move to this new digital landscape. Digital technology has made it easier than ever for people to create and distribute their own content, which challenges the dominance of traditional publishing houses. I did really appreciate the tension that Ludovico and Cramer discusses that there is still a fundamental desire for the physical object. This has led to a resurgence of interest in print books and a renewed appreciation for the art of bookmaking.
Week 5

Duvall's proposition of our move into hyper modernism really made me think about its current place in the digital world right now. I agree with his stance that hyper modernism is alive and well and I feel like it can be seen through UX/UI best practices, graphic design formats, iconography etc. Through classes and honestly looking at the way the digital world is formatted now, I can see a standardization of the digital world and a push for what is right and what is considered "not".
Week 6

Prior to this reading, I was not as familiar with gestalt principles and their influence on design (mostly just psychology). Gestalt principles have had a significant influence on graphic design by providing a framework for organizing and structuring visual elements to create effective and engaging designs. 'Intention' was something that was a key takeaway for me through this reading. An artist intensively uses principles like proximity, similarity, figure/ground to evoke certain feelings and by that artists can create designs that are not only aesthetically pleasing, but also effective when conveying their intended message or meaning to the viewer.
Week 8

I never thought of alt-text in the context of poetry but it makes so much sense. With poetry, the words written mean so much more than just its presence. Each word is supposed to illicit a feeling, thought, or imagery in your head. Alt-text serves as the same thing for people with accessibility needs. When using alt-text we are able to give those who need it a translation of our visuals. I also think that treating alt-text as poetry opens up a new creative world to the art of writing it.

Week 13

Soulellis also raises important questions about the implications of this feed-based culture, particularly in terms of how it affects our attention spans and our ability to engage with information in a meaningful way. He suggests that feeds have a "performative" quality, in that they encourage us to skim and scroll through content rather than engaging with it deeply. This can be seen as a form of "hyperconsumption," where we are constantly bombarded with information but rarely take the time to reflect on it or form our own opinions. This stood out to me because it paralleled a conversation I was having with my friend the other day, where she said that social media has induced ADHD in GenZ
